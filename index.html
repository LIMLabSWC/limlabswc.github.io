Finding and exploiting patterns and regularities in the environment is a critical brain function for animals living in a complex yet structured world. Individuals can deduce abstract relations, learn these regularities through experience, and use prior information to guide future behaviour. How do animals infer these patterns and relations, and how are priors formed, represented, and stored?

Our past, present, and future are intimately linked by our memories of various timescales. The brain is constantly engaged in storing new memories and executing actions while integrating incoming sensory input with past memories and internal models of the world. What are the fundamental principles of sensory memory organisation and their utilisation in inference problems? How are learned priors integrated in memory, or used with new sensory information?

At LIM Lab we employ a synergistic combination of theory and experiment to study neuronal computations underlying sensory memory organisation and sensory inference. We use high-throughput training to combine sophisticated, well-controlled behavioural paradigms with powerful tools to monitor and manipulate neural circuits. Experiments are intertwined with hypotheses from theory and computational models.
